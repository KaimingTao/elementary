# Elementary questions



Three patterns of questions I am interested in,

- how to think
- what to do
- how to do

Throughout the short human history, people from all time and all places created a myriad of theories, principles, methods to answer some concrete or abstract versions of these questions. I read some of them, I agree some of them, and I also see the limitations in some of them.

As one of the people who prefers to seek some potentially better ideas, I took notes every time when I learnt or thought about some good ideas. Throughout the time, I realized some of them provide good paths of thinking and help me solve real world problems. As these patterns of questions are so common and the solutions are generally useful, I decide to share with people who are thinking about questions like **how to think about this?**, **what should I do?**, **how I can achieve the goal?**

I consider these ideas are *elementary*, one reason as mentioned above is they're generalized methods from some logical reasoning. Another reason is I also think these ideas can be the basic for *any kind of intelligence*, human, machine, extraterrestrial, society, company, etc.

Some ideas you might be familiar with because I learnt from some well-known people. For others are my insights, you may find they are strange or may be hard to understand, you may agree or disagree. My goal is not to find some absolutely right ideas, my goal is to find ideas that can help solve real world questions. If the idea works that's good, if not I prefer to learn from others and get more experience, I consider it's open-minded. Many times I see solving a problem is just **change the way of thinking**.

I think with enough time of experiencing and thinking, people can realize these ideas by themselves, but some thinking barriers are natural in the thinking pathway, and passing through them are not easy. Even understanding some well-know good ideas requires enough experience and time, for some people it's decades, for some people it will be never. I also see some barriers will repeat everywhere, and share similar patterns. So I note down some methods that I know can help pass the barriers, they are good tips and tricks for myself. To be noted, I don't know all methods.

These ideas are fragmented. They randomly showed in front of me during years of learning and I wrote them down in pieces, so they're not in a systematic way. Many times I tried to organize them in different structures and the main problem is **there're always some cases the organized knowledge is not useful**. Also my good-idea-collections are still evolving and expanding. To be honest I doubt there exists a best way, so I give up to assume the best way exists.

I organized the ideas in **idea cards**. One idea card contains **main idea**, **bullet points** which are related sub ideas, and **some comments** for explanation. They're like Lego blocks or puzzle pieces. When I realized I can make them better I will update the bullet points, rewrite comments, or split the idea to idea cards with more details, or combine idea cards to one if I learn a new way to combine them smoothly, or I'll mark the limitations I learn. I realize one of my goal is to make idea cards concise, so I can use them as tips, and I won't confuse myself, I'm also my audience. The other goal is making the thinking fun.

An article is a linear media format, but the ideas exist in multiple forms of thinking network, a work that organize these ideas in a linear way will lost some information for readability, even the typesetting will affect understanding. On the other hand, a structured article is a good guide for thinking. In the future I may try to organize some ideas into media formats like articles, audios, videos. The work feels like using elementary pieces to create a fancy house. Building work can help me get deeper (high level) understanding. Also in this way, a more complex or complicated ideas will emerge and it would be a new powerful tool.

I'm continuously learning and thinking new ideas, so this document will be a working progress. Some complex ways of thinking may be included, I may overturn some ideas if I find it's not pragmatic. This is fun and will help me be better.


> The real voyage of discovery consists not in seeking new landscapes but in having new eyes.

### IPO model

It's called IPO model because originally the three labels are input, process, output. I change the label in first person perspective.

![IPO model](./IPO%20model.png)

- **sense** is to collect data from environment
    - I also call it *perception, feeling*,  I prefer to use verb
- **think** is to process and analyzing the data from perception, then make decisions
    - here the *mind* exists
- **action** is to do something
    - also could be *reaction*, as feedback to the environment

This model is what I consider **a basic model**, it helps to split hard questions to simpler ones. I see it's a basic principle of how things are structured and connected.

BTW, my definition is for practical usage, and I'm not planning to define them as a philosophical term.

### Environment

an additional piece for IPO model

- also called **context**, **background**
- when a group of objects are in consideration, there could be some subjects and the environment
    - for a phone you are using, the phone is the subject, other phones and humans are the environment
    - for a car you are driving, it's the subject, other cars, pedestrians, motorcycles, etc., construct the environment
    - for a person, family, community, house, nature, country can be the environment
    - we can consider ourselves as part of the environment, that is retrospective thinking
- the purpose of using **environment** is to have a scope of thinking, and have a clear definition of subject
    - if the scope is unclear, it leads to confusion
    - **I'm drinking a cup of coffee**
        - in my house, or in a cafe, or using good quality coffee beans, or using coffee powder
            - all mean different things
        - determine the context help us understand what happened
        - and reduce confusion
- context is not always obvious
    - which leads to confusion
- context is dynamic, it's changing, know how it changes helps
